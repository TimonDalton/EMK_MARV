P3.1 Introduction
In this practical, you will develop (i) a communication system that implements serial communication in the form of RS232 and I2C protocols and (ii) transfer and store data between a PC or mobile device and the microcontroller and between the microcontroller and a serial EEPROM.

You will communicate with the PIC by sending and receiving text commands via the EUSART from a PC or mobile device. All text responses generated by the PIC must be stored in an external EEPROM connected through an I²C bus. These stored responses should be reprogrammable whenever required.
P3.1.1 Learning objectives  
You will learn and understand 
the basics about how communication between a µC and an external device, whether it be a memory chip, a PC or a mobile device, is implemented, 
how to use lookup tables to store text messages, 
what could go wrong in an embedded system, and
how to integrate several subsystems into a functional system.

P3.1.2 Where will we learn all this?

You will build on the knowledge and experience you have gained from the design and implementation of Practicals 1 and 2. 

Chapter 8 of MARVellous Micros looks at 
how data such as text messages and calibration data may be stored in tables, and
how macros can be used to simplify the implementation of specific functions.

Chapter 9 explains serial communication through a universal synchronous/asynchronous receiver/transmitter (USART) using the RS232 protocol and through a master synchronous serial port (MSSP) using the I2C protocol. Code examples in the chapter cover
program branches and how the memory addresses for these branches are calculated (which is important for a true understanding of the µC's program execution), 
setting up a delay loop, which depends on program branches and subroutines, 
external interrupts and interrupts on change (IOC),
RS232 communication, and
I2C communication. 


Chapter 11 of MARVellous Micros addresses a variety of issues that may cause your MARV to fail, and that one should consider when developing a product that uses an embedded system.

P3.2.2 Communication, data transfer and storage (CDTS) subsystem
P3.2.2.1 System description
The first function of the CDTS system is to establish a communication channel between a PC or mobile device and the PIC18F45K22 microcontroller using the PIC’s onboard EUSART. This channel enables the implementation of a user interface (UI) through which system modes and other data can be transmitted to the PIC.
One of the following methods may be used to establish this connection:
A USB-to-serial interface can be implemented using the PIC18F14K50 microcontroller, the MCP2200 USB-to-UART converter, the MCP2221A USB-to-I2C/UART converter, or a commercially available USB-to-serial bridge.
Communication with the PC can be performed using a standard serial terminal application, such as PuTTY.
Alternatively, a WiFi-to-serial bridge may be implemented using the ESP-01 WiFi module. 
Wireless communication can then be achieved using either a standard WiFi-to-serial application or a custom-developed mobile application (for a bonus mark).
The second function of the CDTS system is to establish communication between the PIC18F45K22 and an external serial EEPROM using the industry-standard I²C protocol. All text responses generated by the PIC in response to commands or communication requests received via the EUSART must be stored in this EEPROM. During operation, the PIC must retrieve the appropriate response data from the EEPROM based on the received commands. In addition, the system must support reprogramming of these responses on demand, requiring both read and write access to the EEPROM for storing and retrieving text data.

P3.2.2.3 Communication interfaces and protocols
Communication between PC/mobile device and PIC
Type:		RS232 asynchronous serial (3-wire cable)
Rate: 		19 200 Baud proposed, but you may choose
Format: 	ASCII
Bits:		8-bit; one start bit; one stop bit; no parity

Ensure that you specify the correct baud rate in your terminal application. Ensure that the correct COM port is selected (you can check this in the PC's Device Manager, which may be accessed through the Control Panel's "System" tab).
Communication between PIC and EEPROM
Type:	I2C (refer to datasheet). 
NB: 	The I2C protocol need not be bit-banged, i.e. you may make use of the on-board I2C 
functionality of the PIC.

P3.4.1.1 Prepare a mini-poster of your MARV
Mini-poster template
Mini-poster submission link

Design an A3 mini-poster for your MARV that showcases its development and the results achieved for its main functions. Both the hardware and firmware should follow a modular design, dividing the system into functional units. Functional units are self-contained subsystems responsible for specific tasks, such as calibration and navigation. This modularity allows each unit to be developed and tested independently.

Your mini-poster should reflect this structure by clearly describing each functional unit, the method used to test it, and the results obtained. A template is provided via the link above; however, you may modify the layout as needed, provided that all required components are included.

Please upload your poster by 14:30 on the day of Practical 3, i.e. at the start of the session.
Ensure that your file is named according to the required format: TeamXX.pdf (e.g., Team100.pdf).
Please note that late submissions cannot be accepted; if your poster is not available at the time of grading, a mark of zero will be awarded. In addition, failure to submit a poster will result in no exam entry being granted.
P3.4.1.2 Grading of miniposters
View the mini-posters 
Mini-poster assessment form


Each student must assess the mini-posters of the next two teams in sequence after their own team. 
For example, if you are in Team 20 and posters have been uploaded for Teams 21 and 23 (but not for Team 22), you must assess Teams 21 and 23. If a team submits their poster late, it is treated as not submitted (no poster = no mark), and you should move on to the next available team. 
For teams at the end of the list, the numbering “rolls over” to the beginning (similar to a timer). For instance, the teams following the last team will continue from Team 1 onward.

All students must complete their peer assessments individually by 23:59 on the day following the practical demonstration. Participation in the peer review process is compulsory. Students who do not complete the peer assessment will receive a mark of zero for their individual poster mark (i.e., no peer review = 0).

Mini-posters will be graded using a 5-point scale based on the clarity, modularity, and completeness of the system presentation:
5 – Excellent: Clear, well-structured poster with a strong modular design. All functional units are clearly described, including testing methods and results. Presentation is professional and easy to follow.
4 – Good: Mostly clear and well-organised. Functional units and testing are described with minor omissions or lack of detail. Overall, a good presentation.
3 – Satisfactory: Adequate description of the system, but the modular structure, testing methods, or results lack clarity or completeness.
2 – Poor: Limited or unclear description of functional units and testing. Poster lacks structure or is difficult to follow.
1 – Very Poor: Minimal or incomplete content. Functional units, testing, and results are largely missing or unclear.

The final poster mark will be determined as follows: 50% from the practical evaluators and 50% from the average peer assessment mark.
P3.4.2 Lab books
Your lab book should not be written as a formal, structured report, nor should it be compiled only for the demonstration. Instead, it should be maintained continuously throughout the project as a working record of your progress.
Include notes on your planning and design decisions, how you implemented your designs, how you tested the system, and what measurements or observations you made. The lab book should also contain a concise summary of your final design and implementation.
The purpose of the lab book is to help you track and document your project development, rather than to serve as a mark-bearing report. If it is kept up to date throughout the project, it will be ready for the evaluator's review during the demonstration.
In addition, include your project budget in the lab book, along with proof of expenses.
